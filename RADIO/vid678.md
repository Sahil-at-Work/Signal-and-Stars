# ⏱ 2:10 – 5:30

# Karl Jansky — The Accidental Discovery

To understand the birth of radio astronomy, we must go back to the early 1930s.

At that time, radio communication technology was rapidly developing.

Telecommunication companies were trying to improve **long-distance radio transmissions**, especially across the Atlantic Ocean.

One of those companies was **Bell Telephone Laboratories** in the United States.

Bell Labs hired a young engineer named **Karl Jansky**.

His job was very practical.

He was asked to investigate a problem.

Radio communication signals were being disturbed by **strange noise**.

These noises interfered with telephone transmissions.

Bell Labs wanted to know:

Where was this noise coming from?

So Jansky designed a very unusual antenna.

It was a **large rotating radio antenna**, about 30 meters wide.

Because of its shape and structure, people jokingly called it **“Jansky’s merry-go-round.”**

The antenna rotated slowly and continuously measured radio signals coming from different directions in the sky.

Jansky began collecting data.

Soon he noticed three different types of radio noise.

The first type came from **nearby thunderstorms**.

The second type came from **distant lightning storms**.

But the third type was very strange.

It appeared **every single day**, at almost the same time.

At first, Jansky assumed the source was the Sun.

But after carefully analyzing the timing of the signal, he realized something remarkable.

The signal repeated every **23 hours and 56 minutes**.

That is not the solar day.

That is the **sidereal day**, the rotation period of the Earth relative to distant stars.

This meant the signal was coming from **outside our solar system**.

By tracking the direction of the signal, Jansky discovered something astonishing.

The radio waves were coming from the **center of our galaxy — the Milky Way**.

For the first time in history, humans had detected **radio emission from space**.

This discovery was made in **1932**.

And it marked the **birth of radio astronomy**.

---

# ⏱ 5:30 – 7:30

# Grote Reber — The First Radio Telescope

Although Jansky made a groundbreaking discovery, Bell Labs was mainly interested in communication technology.

They did not continue this astronomical research.

But someone else became fascinated by Jansky’s results.

His name was **Grote Reber**.

Reber was an amateur radio engineer and a passionate scientist.

After reading Jansky’s research papers, he decided to continue the investigation himself.

But there was one problem.

No radio telescope existed.

So Reber decided to build one.

In **1937**, he constructed the **first parabolic radio telescope** in his own backyard in Illinois.

Imagine that.

The world’s first radio telescope was not built by a government agency or a large observatory.

It was built by one person, working at home.

The telescope was about **9 meters in diameter**, shaped like a parabolic dish.

This design allowed radio waves to be reflected and focused into a receiver.

Using this telescope, Reber began mapping radio signals coming from the sky.

Over several years, he created the **first radio map of the Milky Way**.

His observations confirmed Jansky’s discovery and showed that many regions of the galaxy emit radio waves.

Because of his work, radio astronomy became a legitimate field of scientific research.

Today, Grote Reber is often called the **father of radio astronomy instrumentation**.

# Ooty Radio Telescope

One of the earliest major radio telescopes built in India is the **Ooty Radio Telescope**, often abbreviated as **ORT**.

It is located in the hills of **Ooty in Tamil Nadu**.

This telescope has a very unique design.

Instead of a circular dish, the Ooty Radio Telescope is a **long cylindrical structure**, about **530 meters long and 30 meters wide**.

It operates primarily at a frequency of **327 MHz**, which lies in the radio part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

The telescope was built along a **hill slope**, which allows it to track celestial objects as the Earth rotates.

The Ooty Radio Telescope has contributed significantly to research in:

* pulsars
* interplanetary scintillation
* and studies of the interstellar medium.

For many years, it was one of the most powerful radio telescopes in the world.

# Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope (GMRT)

Perhaps the most famous radio astronomy facility in India today is the **Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope**, or **GMRT**.

The GMRT is located near **Khodad, about 80 kilometers from Pune**.

Unlike a single dish telescope, GMRT is an **interferometric array**.

It consists of **30 large parabolic antennas**, each about **45 meters in diameter**.

These antennas are spread across a region of about **25 kilometers**.

By combining signals from multiple antennas, GMRT can achieve the resolution of a much larger telescope.

GMRT operates primarily in the **metre wavelength range**, which makes it ideal for studying:

* hydrogen gas in galaxies
* pulsars
* radio galaxies
* and the large-scale structure of the universe.

In recent years, GMRT has undergone a major upgrade and is now known as **UGMRT — the upgraded GMRT**, with improved receivers and sensitivity.

# ⏱ 1:40 – 3:10

# Ooty Radio Telescope (ORT)

Let us begin with the **Ooty Radio Telescope**, located in **Ooty, Tamil Nadu, India**.

This telescope was built in the early **1970s** under the leadership of **Professor Govind Swarup**.

The Ooty Radio Telescope has a very unique structure.

Instead of being a circular dish, it is a **long cylindrical parabolic antenna**.

The telescope is approximately:

* **530 meters long**
* **30 meters wide**

This makes it one of the largest radio telescopes built during its time.

Another interesting feature is that the telescope is constructed **along a hill slope**.

This allows the telescope to track celestial objects as the Earth rotates.

The Ooty Radio Telescope operates primarily at a frequency of **327 MHz**, which lies in the **meter wavelength region** of the radio spectrum.

Some important scientific studies performed using ORT include:

* observations of **pulsars**
* studies of the **interstellar medium**
* **interplanetary scintillation**, which helps scientists study the solar wind.

Even today, the Ooty Radio Telescope continues to contribute valuable data to radio astronomy research.

# ⏱ 3:10 – 4:40

# Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope (GMRT)

The next telescope we will discuss is the **Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope**, commonly known as **GMRT**.

This telescope is located near **Khodad, about 80 kilometers from Pune, India**.

GMRT is one of the **largest and most sensitive radio telescope arrays operating at meter wavelengths**.

Instead of a single dish, GMRT consists of **30 large parabolic antennas**, each with a diameter of **45 meters**.

These antennas are distributed across an area of about **25 kilometers**.

By combining signals from multiple antennas, GMRT acts as a giant **interferometric array**.

This technique allows astronomers to achieve extremely high angular resolution.

GMRT operates over a frequency range roughly between **150 MHz and 1420 MHz**, covering important parts of the radio spectrum.

It is widely used for studying:

* **neutral hydrogen gas in galaxies**
* **pulsars**
* **radio galaxies**
* **large-scale structure of the universe**

Recently, GMRT was upgraded to **UGMRT**, which improved its receivers and increased its sensitivity significantly.

Today, astronomers from all around the world use GMRT for cutting-edge radio astronomy research.

# ⏱ 4:40 – 6:20

# FAST Telescope (China)

Another remarkable radio telescope is the **FAST telescope**, located in **Guizhou Province, China**.

FAST stands for **Five-hundred-meter Aperture Spherical Telescope**.

As the name suggests, the telescope has a **diameter of about 500 meters**, making it the **largest single-dish radio telescope in the world**.

FAST was completed in **2016**.

The telescope is built inside a **natural karst depression**, which helps support its massive structure.

The surface of FAST consists of **thousands of adjustable panels** that can change shape slightly to focus incoming radio waves.

This design allows the telescope to observe different regions of the sky.

FAST operates roughly between **70 MHz and 3 GHz**, covering a wide range of radio frequencies.

Because of its enormous collecting area, FAST is extremely sensitive.

It is used for studies such as:

* discovering new **pulsars**
* detecting **fast radio bursts**
* mapping **hydrogen gas in galaxies**
* searching for signals that might indicate **extraterrestrial intelligence**.

---

# ⏱ 6:20 – 7:40

# Very Large Array (VLA)

The **Very Large Array**, or **VLA**, is located in **New Mexico, United States**.

Unlike FAST, which is a single giant dish, the VLA is an **interferometric array** consisting of **27 radio antennas**.

Each antenna has a diameter of **25 meters**.

These antennas are arranged in a **Y-shaped configuration** across the desert.

One fascinating feature of the VLA is that the antennas can **move along railway tracks**.

This allows scientists to change the distance between antennas, adjusting the telescope’s **resolution and field of view**.

The VLA operates across a wide range of frequencies, roughly between **1 GHz and 50 GHz**.

It has been responsible for many major discoveries, including:

* detailed imaging of **radio galaxies**
* studies of **black hole jets**
* mapping of **star-forming regions**
* and observations of **supernova remnants**.

Because of its flexibility and high resolution, the VLA remains one of the most powerful radio observatories in the world.

# Comparison of Major Radio Telescopes

Now let us briefly compare these telescopes.

### Size

FAST is currently the **largest single-dish radio telescope**, with a diameter of **500 meters**.

The Ooty Radio Telescope is extremely long but narrow, measuring **530 meters by 30 meters**.

GMRT uses **30 dishes of 45 meters each**, forming a large interferometric array.

The VLA uses **27 antennas of 25 meters each**.

---

### Frequency Range

Different telescopes operate in different parts of the radio spectrum.

* Ooty Radio Telescope: around **327 MHz**
* GMRT: approximately **150 MHz to 1420 MHz**
* FAST: about **70 MHz to 3 GHz**
* VLA: about **1 GHz to 50 GHz**

Each frequency range is useful for studying different astrophysical phenomena.

---

### Scientific Goals

Each telescope is designed with specific scientific objectives.

For example:

Ooty Radio Telescope focuses on **pulsars and interplanetary scintillation**.

GMRT is particularly powerful for studying **hydrogen gas and radio galaxies**.

FAST is used for **extremely sensitive surveys**, especially discovering new pulsars.

The VLA excels at **high-resolution imaging of galaxies and black hole jets**.

Together, these telescopes help astronomers explore different aspects of the universe.
